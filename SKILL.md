# Regional Eating Patterns & Meal Planning Skills

## Purpose
This document serves as a reference for the meal planning AI to understand authentic regional eating habits, preventing bad assumptions (e.g., serving Curd Rice for breakfast when it's a South Indian lunch item).

## Indian Regional Patterns

### North Indian
- Breakfast: paratha (aloo/gobi/methi), poha, upma, chole bhature (weekend), aloo puri, bread-omelette, besan chilla, stuffed kulcha, chai
- Lunch: roti + dal + sabzi + rice (optional) + raita/salad + pickle, rajma-chawal, kadhi-chawal, chole with rice or bhature
- Dinner: lighter than lunch — roti + sabzi + dal, khichdi, daliya (broken wheat porridge), soup + roti in winter
- NOT breakfast: biryani, curd rice, heavy curries, rajma without bread, paneer butter masala
- Regional notes: Punjab is heavier (makki roti + sarson ka saag in winter), UP/Bihar lighter (sattu paratha, litti chokha for lunch). Dinner is almost always lighter than lunch.

### South Indian
- Breakfast: idli + sambar/chutney, dosa (masala/plain/rava/set), medu vada (weekend/special), pongal (ven/sakkarai), upma, puttu + kadala curry (Kerala), appam + stew (Kerala), pesarattu (Andhra), uttapam
- Lunch: rice + sambar + rasam + poriyal/kootu + curd + papad + pickle (Tamil Nadu meals format), Andhra meals add avakaya pickle + pappu + pachadi, Kerala sadya format for feasts
- Dinner: dosa/idli/upma (lighter), or rice + lighter curry, chapati + kurma, lemon rice, curd rice (comfort/end-of-meal)
- NOT breakfast: heavy non-veg curries, biryani, curd rice (it is a lunch/end-of-meal item), fish curry
- Regional notes: Curd rice (thayir sadam) is specifically a South Indian lunch closer/comfort food, never breakfast. Pesarattu is Andhra-specific. Kerala breakfast is distinct (puttu, appam, idiyappam).

### East Indian (Bengali/Odia/Assamese)
- Breakfast: luchi + aloo dum, paratha, panta bhat (fermented rice with onion/chili — summer only, rural), muri (puffed rice) + tea, roti + sabzi
- Lunch: rice + dal (musur/moong) + fish curry + sabzi + chutney (the classic bhaat-mach-dal), shukto (bitter vegetable stew as first course), begun bhaja (fried eggplant), doi (curd)
- Dinner: roti/rice + lighter preparations, moong dal khichdi, fish if not had at lunch
- NOT breakfast: fish curry, heavy rice-based meals, full Bengali thali
- Regional notes: Fish is central to Bengali lunch but NOT breakfast. Odia cuisine shares similarities but includes dalma (lentils with vegetables). Assamese food features khar (alkaline dish) and tenga (sour fish curry) at lunch.

### West Indian (Gujarati/Maharashtrian/Rajasthani)
- Breakfast: thepla (Gujarati), dhokla, poha (Maharashtrian staple), upma, misal pav (Maharashtra), sabudana khichdi (fasting-friendly), khandvi (Gujarati), dal-pakwan (Sindhi)
- Lunch: Gujarati thali (dal + kadhi + rice + roti + sabzi + papad + pickle + sweet item like shrikhand), Maharashtrian (varan-bhaat + usal/sabzi + bhakri), Rajasthani (dal-baati-churma, gatte ki sabzi + roti)
- Dinner: khichdi, lighter dal-roti, pav bhaji (street food style), thepla with pickle
- NOT breakfast: undhiyu (elaborate Gujarati mixed veg — lunch/dinner), puran poli (festival sweet — not everyday), heavy Rajasthani curries
- Regional notes: Gujarati food has a distinctive sweet note in savory dishes. Maharashtra has strong street food culture (vada pav, misal pav) but these are snacks/breakfast, not dinner.

## East Asian Patterns

### Chinese
- Breakfast: congee/zhou (rice porridge with preserved egg, pork floss, or pickles), youtiao (fried dough sticks), jianbing (savory crepe), baozi (steamed buns), soy milk (doujiang), tea eggs, scallion pancakes (cong you bing), xiao long bao (Shanghai)
- Lunch: noodle soups (beef noodle, wonton noodle), fried rice, stir-fry dishes with steamed rice, jiaozi (dumplings), mapo tofu with rice
- Dinner: multi-dish family style (3-4 dishes sharing + rice + soup), hot pot (communal/weekend), steamed fish, braised meats, stir-fried vegetables
- NOT breakfast: hot pot, elaborate multi-dish meals, Peking duck, heavy braised dishes
- Regional notes: Northern China breakfast is heartier (jianbing, baozi). Southern/Cantonese breakfast is dim sum culture (har gow, siu mai, cheung fun — but this is a weekend/social affair, not daily). Sichuan adds spice even at breakfast (dan dan noodles). Congee is breakfast/late-night comfort food, not a dinner main.

### Japanese
- Breakfast: steamed rice + miso soup + grilled fish (salmon/mackerel) + pickles (tsukemono) + natto + tamagoyaki (rolled omelette) + nori (seaweed). This is traditional "ichiju-issai" (one soup, one side) or richer "ichiju-sansai" (one soup, three sides). Modern: toast + egg + coffee (yoshoku style).
- Lunch: bento boxes (rice + protein + sides + pickles), ramen, udon, soba, donburi (gyudon, katsudon, oyakodon), onigiri + miso soup, curry rice
- Dinner: ichiju-sansai format (1 soup + 3 sides + rice), sashimi/sushi, grilled fish, nabemono (hot pot in winter), tempura, yakitori, tonkatsu
- NOT breakfast: ramen (it is lunch/late-night), sushi/sashimi, heavy fried dishes, curry rice
- Regional notes: Kansai (Osaka) is known for takoyaki/okonomiyaki but these are street food/dinner, not breakfast. Hokkaido features dairy and seafood more prominently. Okinawan diet is distinct (goya champuru, purple sweet potato).

### Korean
- Breakfast: rice + soup (doenjang jjigae, miyeok-guk/seaweed soup, kongnamul-guk/bean sprout soup) + banchan (kimchi, namul, jangajji) + egg. Simpler than lunch/dinner. Juk (porridge) for lighter mornings.
- Lunch: bibimbap, jjigae (kimchi jjigae, sundubu), gimbap, naengmyeon (summer), kalguksu (knife-cut noodles), dolsot-bap (stone pot rice)
- Dinner: grilled meats (samgyeopsal, galbi, bulgogi) + rice + banchan + soup/jjigae, jjimdak (braised chicken), haemul-tang (seafood stew), dakgalbi
- NOT breakfast: samgyeopsal/grilled meat, heavy stews as standalone, elaborate BBQ setup
- Regional notes: Banchan appears at every meal but variety increases lunch-to-dinner. Jeju island has more seafood (haenyeo/diver culture). Jeonju is bibimbap capital. Korean breakfast is declining among younger generation (replaced by toast/coffee).

### Thai
- Breakfast: jok (rice congee with egg/pork), khao tom (rice soup), pa thong ko (fried dough with condensed milk/pandan custard), kai dao (fried egg on rice), moo ping (grilled pork skewers) from street stalls
- Lunch: pad thai, curries (green/red/massaman) with jasmine rice, som tum (papaya salad) + sticky rice + grilled chicken (Isaan set), noodle soups (boat noodles, tom yum noodle), khao pad (fried rice)
- Dinner: shared family-style — curry + stir-fry + soup + rice, or single-plate street food. Similar to lunch but often more elaborate at home.
- NOT breakfast: elaborate multi-curry spreads, heavy coconut-based curries, som tum (it is lunch/afternoon)
- Regional notes: Isaan (northeast) food is distinct — sticky rice is the staple (not jasmine), heavy use of fermented fish (pla ra), larb and som tum. Southern Thai is spicier and more coconut-heavy. Bangkok street breakfast culture is strong (jok and pa thong ko stalls open at 5 AM).

### Vietnamese
- Breakfast: pho (beef or chicken noodle soup — the quintessential breakfast), banh mi (baguette sandwich with pate, cold cuts, pickled veg), xoi (sticky rice with toppings), bun (vermicelli noodle soups), banh cuon (steamed rice rolls)
- Lunch: com tam (broken rice + grilled pork + egg + pickles — Saigon staple), bun cha (Hanoi — grilled pork + noodles + herbs), banh xeo (crispy crepe), bo kho (beef stew with bread)
- Dinner: family-style — canh (clear soup) + stir-fry/braised dish + steamed rice + fresh herbs, or hot pot (lau) for gatherings
- NOT breakfast: elaborate hot pot, heavy braised dishes, banh xeo (it is lunch/dinner)
- Regional notes: Northern (Hanoi) food is subtler and less sweet. Southern (Saigon) food is sweeter with more herbs. Central (Hue) food is spicier and more elaborate (bun bo Hue). Pho is truly a breakfast food in Vietnam, eaten before 9 AM at street stalls.

## Western Patterns

### American
- Breakfast: eggs (scrambled/fried/omelette/benedict), pancakes/waffles with maple syrup, oatmeal, cereal with milk, toast/bagel with cream cheese, bacon/sausage, smoothie/acai bowl, hash browns, breakfast burrito (Southwest)
- Lunch: sandwiches (club, BLT, grilled cheese), salads (Caesar, Cobb), soups (clam chowder, tomato), wraps, burgers, grain bowls
- Dinner: protein + starch + vegetable (meat-and-potatoes tradition), pasta dishes, grilled/roasted meats, casseroles, tacos (Taco Tuesday culture), pizza
- NOT breakfast: steak dinner, heavy pasta dishes, casseroles, roasted meats
- Regional notes: Southern US has distinct breakfast (biscuits and gravy, grits, country ham). Southwest/Tex-Mex influence (breakfast tacos, huevos rancheros). Pacific Northwest is seafood-heavy. Midwest is casserole/comfort food culture.

### European (Continental)
- Breakfast: bread + butter + jam + cheese + cold cuts + coffee (Germany/Netherlands), croissant + coffee (France), toast + beans + egg + sausage (UK full English — weekend), porridge (Scandinavia/UK)
- Lunch: main meal of the day in many countries — Germany (Mittagessen: schnitzel + potato + salad), France (plat du jour: protein + side + salad), Italy (primo: pasta, secondo: protein + contorno: vegetable)
- Dinner: lighter in Southern/Central Europe — soup, bread, salad, cold plates. UK dinner is heavier (roast dinners, pies, fish and chips). Scandinavian: open-faced sandwiches (smorrebrod), lighter fare.
- NOT breakfast: heavy roasts, elaborate multi-course meals, rich stews
- Regional notes: Mediterranean countries eat dinner late (9-10 PM Spain, 8-9 PM Italy). Northern Europe eats earlier (6-7 PM). French lunch is sacred (1-2 hours). German Abendbrot (evening bread) is literally cold bread + cheese + meat for dinner.

### Mediterranean
- Breakfast: bread + olive oil + tomatoes (pan con tomate — Spain), cheese + olives + cucumber + tomato (Turkey/Greece), shakshuka (eggs in tomato sauce — Israel/North Africa), labneh + za'atar + pita (Levant), bougatsa (Greece)
- Lunch: salad + grilled protein + bread + hummus/mezze (Levant), pasta/risotto (Italy), paella (Spain — actually lunch, not dinner), moussaka, grilled fish + vegetables
- Dinner: similar to lunch but lighter in many areas, seafood-forward in coastal regions, tapas/small plates (Spain), meze spread (Turkey/Greece/Lebanon)
- NOT breakfast: elaborate mezze spreads (that is lunch/dinner), paella, moussaka, heavy lamb dishes
- Regional notes: The "Mediterranean diet" is not one cuisine — it spans Spain, Italy, Greece, Turkey, Lebanon, Morocco. Olive oil is the unifying ingredient. Meals are social events. Lunch is typically the largest meal.

## Mexican/Latin American

### Mexican
- Breakfast: huevos rancheros (fried eggs on tortilla with salsa), chilaquiles (torn tortillas in salsa with egg/chicken), breakfast burritos, molletes (open-faced bean/cheese on bolillo), tamales with atole (warm corn drink), pan dulce (sweet bread) + coffee, fruit with chili-lime
- Lunch: comida corrida (the main meal, 2-4 PM) — sopa aguada (broth soup) + sopa seca (rice/pasta) + guisado (main dish: mole, enchiladas, chiles rellenos) + beans + tortillas + agua fresca
- Dinner: lighter — tacos (street-style), quesadillas, tamales, pozole (weekends), tlayudas (Oaxaca), or leftovers from comida
- NOT breakfast: mole (elaborate lunch dish), pozole (weekend dinner/celebration), heavy enchilada platters
- Regional notes: Oaxacan cuisine is the most complex (7 moles, tlayudas, chapulines). Yucatan has Mayan influence (cochinita pibil, papadzules). Northern Mexico is more meat-heavy (carne asada, burritos, machaca). Mexico City street food culture is unmatched.

### South American
- Breakfast: arepa (Venezuela/Colombia — corn cake with fillings), empanada, pao de queijo/pan de yuca (cheese bread — Brazil/Ecuador), acai bowl (Brazil), medialunas (Argentine croissants) + cafe con leche, calentado (Colombia — reheated rice + beans + egg)
- Lunch: the main meal — arroz con pollo, rice + beans + protein + salad (Brazilian "prato feito"), bandeja paisa (Colombia — beans, rice, meat, egg, plantain, avocado, arepa), ceviche (Peru — lunch, never dinner), lomo saltado (Peru)
- Dinner: lighter — soup (sancocho, locro), arepas, empanadas, or lighter version of lunch
- NOT breakfast: ceviche (it is strictly a lunch item in Peru), heavy stews, elaborate grilled meat spreads
- Regional notes: Argentine dinner is late (9-10 PM) and meat-heavy (asado culture). Brazilian churrasco is weekend/social. Peruvian cuisine is highly diverse (coastal ceviche, Andean quinoa/potato dishes, Amazonian ingredients). Colombian meals are hearty and portion-heavy.

## African Patterns

### East African (Kenya/Ethiopia/Tanzania)
- Breakfast: mandazi (fried dough) + chai (Kenya/Tanzania), injera + firfir (leftover stew-soaked injera — Ethiopia), uji (porridge), chapati + egg
- Lunch: ugali + sukuma wiki (collard greens) + nyama choma (grilled meat) or stew (Kenya), injera + wot (lentil/meat stew, served on communal injera platter — Ethiopia), pilau (spiced rice)
- Dinner: similar to lunch but can be lighter, nyama choma with kachumbari (fresh salad), Ethiopian dinner is same format as lunch (injera + wots)
- NOT breakfast: ugali (it is lunch/dinner), nyama choma, elaborate wot spreads

### West African (Nigeria/Ghana/Senegal)
- Breakfast: akara (bean fritters) + pap/ogi (fermented corn porridge) — Nigeria, hausa koko (spiced millet porridge) + koose (bean cakes) — Ghana, bread + butter + tea
- Lunch: jollof rice + grilled/fried protein (the iconic dish), fufu/pounded yam + egusi/ogbono/pepper soup (Nigeria), banku + tilapia + pepper (Ghana), thieboudienne (Senegal — fish rice)
- Dinner: amala + ewedu + gbegiri (Yoruba), eba + soup, suya (spiced grilled meat — street food/evening), or lighter versions of lunch
- NOT breakfast: fufu, jollof rice, heavy soups, amala
- Regional notes: West African soups (egusi, ogbono, groundnut) are thick and eaten with starchy sides (fufu, eba, banku). Jollof rice rivalry between Nigeria and Ghana is cultural. Suya is evening/street food.

### North African (Morocco/Tunisia/Egypt)
- Breakfast: msemen/rghaif (flatbread) + honey + cheese + mint tea (Morocco), ful medames (stewed fava beans — Egypt), shakshuka, brik (stuffed pastry — Tunisia), khobz (bread) + olive oil
- Lunch: couscous + tagine (Morocco — Friday couscous is tradition), koshari (Egypt — rice + lentils + pasta + tomato sauce), grilled meats + salad
- Dinner: harira (soup — especially during Ramadan), lighter tagines, grilled meats, salads
- NOT breakfast: tagine, couscous, heavy stewed meats

## Russian/Central Asian

### Russian
- Breakfast: kasha (buckwheat/oat porridge), blini (thin pancakes with sour cream/jam), syrniki (cottage cheese pancakes), buterbrod (open sandwich with cheese/sausage), tvorog (cottage cheese) with honey
- Lunch: three-course tradition — soup first (borsch, shchi/cabbage soup, solyanka, ukha/fish soup), then main (kotlety/cutlets, pelmeni, beef stroganoff) + side (potatoes, kasha, vegetables), then kompot (fruit drink) or tea
- Dinner: lighter — salads (vinegret, Olivier), zakuski (appetizer spreads), pelmeni, soup if not had at lunch, pirozhki (stuffed pastries)
- NOT breakfast: borsch, pelmeni (too heavy), elaborate salad spreads
- Regional notes: Soup at lunch is nearly mandatory in traditional Russian eating. Georgian cuisine (khachapuri, khinkali) is hugely popular across Russia. Uzbek plov (pilaf) is a Central Asian staple for celebrations. Fermented foods (kefir, kvass, sauerkraut) are daily staples.

### Central Asian (Uzbek/Kazakh)
- Breakfast: non (flatbread) + cheese + jam + tea, kaymak (clotted cream), kurt (dried yogurt balls), fried eggs
- Lunch: plov/osh (the national dish — rice pilaf with lamb, carrots, chickpeas), lagman (hand-pulled noodle soup), samsa (baked meat pastries), shurpa (meat and vegetable soup)
- Dinner: manti (steamed dumplings), kebabs (shashlik), lighter soups, non + accompaniments
- NOT breakfast: plov (it is celebratory lunch/special occasion), heavy lamb dishes

## Middle Eastern Patterns

### Levantine (Lebanon/Syria/Jordan/Palestine)
- Breakfast: labneh (strained yogurt) + za'atar + olive oil + pita, hummus + foul (fava beans), halloumi, manakish (za'atar flatbread), olives, cucumber, tomato, fresh mint tea
- Lunch: main meal — grilled meats (shawarma, kebab, kofta) + rice + salad + hummus + fattoush, stuffed grape leaves, kibbeh, musakhan (sumac chicken)
- Dinner: lighter — meze spread (hummus, baba ganoush, tabbouleh, fattoush) + pita, or lighter version of lunch
- NOT breakfast: heavy meat platters, mansaf, elaborate kibbeh preparations

### Gulf (Saudi/Emirati/Omani)
- Breakfast: balaleet (sweet vermicelli + egg — Emirati), liver with spices, foul, labneh, khameer bread, dates + Arabic coffee (always)
- Lunch: kabsa/machboos (spiced rice with meat — the main dish), harees (wheat porridge with meat), saloona (meat stew), grilled meats
- Dinner: lighter — soup, grilled items, or leftover kabsa, dates + yogurt
- NOT breakfast: kabsa, elaborate rice dishes, heavy stews

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Curd Rice is NOT a breakfast item** — it is a South Indian lunch closer/comfort food
- **Biryani is NEVER breakfast** — it is lunch/dinner, often celebratory
- **Heavy curries are NOT breakfast** — paneer butter masala, butter chicken, etc. are lunch/dinner
- **Sattu drink is regional** (Bihar/eastern UP) — not universally Indian
- **Congee is breakfast in China**, NOT dinner (though it can be late-night comfort food)
- **Miso soup is part of Japanese breakfast**, not standalone lunch
- **Pho is breakfast in Vietnam** — it is eaten before 9 AM, not dinner
- **Ceviche is strictly lunch in Peru** — never served at dinner (acid "cooks" the fish, must be fresh)
- **Paella is Spanish lunch**, not dinner
- **Ramen is Japanese lunch/late-night**, not breakfast
- **Ugali is NOT breakfast** in East Africa — it is lunch/dinner
- **Fufu/pounded yam is NOT breakfast** in West Africa
- **Natto is specifically Japanese breakfast** — rarely eaten at other meals
- **Chilaquiles are Mexican breakfast** — not lunch/dinner
- **German Abendbrot is literally cold bread for dinner** — do not generate elaborate hot German dinners
- **Croissant is French breakfast** — not a lunch/dinner component
- **Suya is Nigerian evening/street food** — not a breakfast or formal lunch item

## Glossary Maintenance Protocol

### Staleness Check (On User Request)
When asked to check glossary staleness:
1. Run cuisine distribution analysis on recipe-glossary-seed.json
2. Flag any cuisine with <15 lunch/dinner recipes as "weak"
3. For weak cuisines, search trusted recipe sites for popular dishes
4. Propose additions with full recipe schema
5. After approval, add to chunk files, run combine-chunks.js, seed to Firestore

### Weekly Auto-Refresh Protocol
Every week (or when instructed):
1. Check if any cuisine has <20 lunch/dinner recipes
2. For underrepresented cuisines, search for 5-10 new recipes from trusted sources
3. Validate: each recipe must be a REAL dish found on at least 2 food websites
4. Add to chunk files, merge, and seed

### Anti-Poisoning Rules
- Every recipe name must be googleable and return real results
- No "fusion" inventions (e.g., "Tandoori Taco Bowl")
- Ingredients must be authentic to the cuisine
- Nutrition info must be realistic (cross-check with USDA or similar)
- Tags (fried, high-sugar, etc.) must be accurate for health filtering
